## What is Power?
**Power** influences who we are and what decisions we make. Its mechanisms inform what we can or can't do. Although it's the key to understanding social relations, power isn't as intuitive as we might typically expect.

![power defines what's possible](https://dillonbelmont.com/03powermyth-assets/POWER.gif)

Think of power like a spirit. It exists all around us and can unknowingly possess actors for better or worse. No one is invulnerable to its effects, though some have more access to direct its abilities than others. Perhaps most importantly, power can reproduce itself by lying dormant in what we take for granted; no action is needed if the rules and expectations shaped by power a hundred years ago are left unchanged.

**It is our job, then, to identify power in all its forms.**

## Accumulation
In today’s public imagination, one interpretation of power conquers all, so let's start there. 

A businessman *gains power* as he rises through the ranks in the company. An empire *amasses power* as its territory expands. Super Mario even *powers up* when he obtains a mushroom. This intangible concept is rationalized as a resource that accumulates like capital at the expense of those without it. The more someone has, the stronger they are. They're able to dominate others.

This makes sense at first. Money and job descriptions seem to be easily measurable and compared. But who has *more* power between an unassuming billionaire and an armed vigilante? Which is more influential between a coordinated propaganda network and one deeply resonant work of art? And is it possible for even a successful CEO to be insulated from racial discrimination or stereotyping? The exchange rate, if there could even be one, is entirely context-dependent. 

The properties we often think of *as* power - wealth, status, or even social connections - are merely a means to an end. They are potential. Until someone does something with it, money is only one of the [[10 - 19 Power/14. Power Capacitors\|14. Power Capacitors]].
