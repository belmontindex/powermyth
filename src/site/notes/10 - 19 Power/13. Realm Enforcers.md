# WORKSPACE
Evidently, power can be hoarded by humans to dominate each other. The forms described, though, all have the potential to outlast their original vessel as an embedded part of society. Laws can remain active indefinitely. Ideologies are imbued in every discussion. It is inevitable for people to legitimize imbalanced power dynamics, then, by merely participating in society. Even in the absence of express intent or “villains,” power is continuously reinforced until upended. With this in mind, we are compelled to move past explicitly antagonistic power to that of which flows more freely without direct purpose. The very basics of what we know, how we are defined, and what options we consider are all found in the invisible permeative forms.

### Institutional
Over 200 years ago, Alexander Hamilton successfully made his case for a central federal bank. Though the argument was far from unanimously decided at the time, very few voices are opposed to the mere existence of the Federal Reserve today. It is taken for granted as part of the country, even though the levers it uses to stabilize the economy focus on the interests of stockholders, or the disproportionately wealthy (Sloan & Podkul, 2021). Established institutions organize society and relieve individuals of certain tasks. We collectively empower universities to decide what should be taught, media what should be covered, government what should be addressed, and so on. The formation of these institutions, though, were founded under the conditions of knowledge that existed at the time of their creation. They are imbued with the ideologies of their creators, and the particular “truths” they enforce relay power (Foucault, 1980). A national economic stabilizer could take a bottom-up approach instead of prioritizing the calmness of markets, but this solution has already been carved out.

Formal categorization, a seemingly neutral action, also dictates how problems are solved. Take the federal government’s response to “homelessness,” which sets the nature of the problem (a lack of housing rather than economic deprivation) and the agency primarily responsible (the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development, or HUD). The reality of the situation naturally dictates an interagency response, resulting in inefficiencies as Health and Human Services, the Veterans Administration, and a number of other agencies must coordinate within entirely distinct frameworks and datasets. The Department of Education and HUD do not even share the same definition of what constitutes “homelessness.” This subjectivization also centers individual responsibility (why is someone homeless?) rather than the instability of an economic system that caused someone to be unsheltered in the first place.

Institutions are unavoidable; they shape how we respond to the world around us. This subtly produces certain behaviors. In theory, the categorization and discipline from institutions should foster an efficiently run society, though this is not to everyone’s benefit equally. As Hill Collins & Bilge (2016) describe, “Different people find themselves encountering different treatment regarding which rules apply to them and how those rules will be implemented.” Law enforcement is a prime example of this. Black people, disproportionately impacted by policing practices, often adapt their actions to reduce potential infractions. It is even a well-trodden experience for parents to give their children “The Talk” to guide behavior around officers. Traffic police do not even need to do anything in order to dictate actions of the public: speed cameras, cruisers on the side of the road, and the ever-lingering threat of a ticket all dissuade people from breaking the law. When there is a constant sense of being watched, as in the Panopticon, individuals self-regulate their actions (Foucault, 1977).

While institutions can seem permanent or infallible, this is not always the case. To continue with the example of law enforcement, there was a public reckoning of its place in society following the 2020 murder of George Floyd and ensuing Black Lives Matter protests. Some jurisdictions even reimagined what policing should look like. Change just does not come easy. Institutions represent stability, a psychological need that individuals may opt to protect in spite of their own powerlessness (Jost, 2018). The constancy of institutions, coupled with narratives that legitimize the status quo as the only option, can take precedent over justice.

### Cultural
The counterpart to the formality of institutions is the set of free-flowing norms, assumptions, and manners expressed through culture. There are no clear guidelines for one to smile at a stranger on the train, as it is just a “normal” thing to do. The socially constructed concept of “normal,” though, has hazardous potential. Racist language like the N-word may be a typical part of a southern adolescent’s vocabulary even before they fully understand the concept of race (Thomas et al., 2024). Human beings are relational, and conformity to group expectations helps people distinguish their roles among each other. We subconsciously learn from our surroundings how it is appropriate to act and what is reasonable to think, reproducing a loop of power as new generations follow suit (Bourdieu, 1998). The context from which one came could make even the most heinous prejudice go unquestioned. It could also make any form of oppression - no matter how big or small - seem natural.

There may not be a particular conduit for this type of power, which makes it more diffuse than ever. As soon as someone is born into a poor family, they are already ascribed certain characteristics, and that individual is likely to grow up with some idea of what is or is not possible for them. Culture informs what the conversation and preferences will be (Lukes, 1974). In a sense, it bears similarity to the discipline generated by institutions, producing particular boundaries in which everyone operates. However, the relational element expands the reach so far that no one is immune to its effects: even those with wealth, for example, are still subject to what they are “supposed” to do for the sake of their social status (Hayward, 2000). Deviating from these norms can come at a significant cost to the individual.

Once more, though, change is possible. Counterculture movements throughout history have demonstrated that people operating outside of norms can shift the needle for the general public. Drag queens call attention to the acceptance of gender fluidity, Civil Rights leaders questioned the treatment of Black people, and feminist groups forced a closer look at the role of the woman. Non-identity countercultures also threaten dominant power, as hip-hop, beatniks, and hippies have all challenged what their contemporaries take for granted and question dominating structures. The underlying hurdle, of course, is the very tall order that countering culture asks. Fundamental beliefs that were seemingly just ingrained from the start are not easy to shake off - assuming someone even wants to in the first place. The root of inequity is found in every human action.

### Permeative Limitations
Just as an accumulative lens of power is incomplete, so too is the permeative. Power’s ubiquity means its exercise is not always intentional; however, the manifestations identified here have also been exploited as strategies to dominate. The assumptions and norms we associate with any particular area leave us vulnerable to manipulation when someone leverages that familiarity as a means to a self-serving end goal. By borrowing baked-in calculations and transferring them to an alternative purpose, one commits mythappropriation. For example, a wealthy CEO might invoke adages of meritocracy and market conditions to justify slashing fair hiring practices or providing a living wage. Religion, American history, and other near-universally understood facets of life are all frequent subjects of mythappropriation, making it one of the least obvious but most prevalent exercises of power. Additionally, norms can just be changed, by force, to more closely align with dominant interests (Klein, 2007). Political crisis in Chile opened the door for Milton Friedman and Augusto Pinochet to use “shock therapy” to redefine the role of the government, reframe inequality as a matter of individual responsibility, and reshape the limits of what was possible in the name of free-market capitalism.

The value from this permeative lens is twofold. First, it removes the expectation that power is only something used to purposely repress someone at a disadvantage. One way or another, everyone is affected by it and everyone generates it. Addressing inequity cannot stop with those who “have” power, like billionaires or political leaders, because it simply exists everywhere, all the time. Second, it reminds us that everything needs to be questioned. Especially for those who are in more privileged positions already, it is likely there are countless practices we take for granted that needlessly hurt or disadvantage someone else. Injustice could literally be anywhere, and of course, that will always be a threat to justice everywhere.
